CPTs

Feb 6, 2016

Semiconductor wafers are processed at fabs with many different types of semiconductor tools, and may undergo several thousand process steps before they are packaged into the final product. Clustered photolithography tools (CPTs) are one of the most complex and expensive types of semiconductor equipment, where a single such tool may cost hundreds of millions of dollars. As such, CPTs are typically the fabricator bottleneck and contribute significantly to the fab cycle time.

" alt="CPT" /> In a single CPT, there are many processing modules and wafers are carried to and from modules by robot arms. In the above configuration, there are four clusters, each with a single-arm robot. Each wafer recipe has its own process flows within a CPT, and setups may be required when changing to another process flow.

There are many ways to model CPTs, ranging from simple linear regression models, to more complex queuing models (called flow lines), all the way to full discrete event simulation models. Each of these models have their pros and cons, and one must consider the performance-computation tradeoff when embedding these equipment models into a full fab simulation.

In J1, I explored how to apply such models to CPTs, developing extensions to certain models, and performed a comprehensive comparison of affine models and flow lines with respect to accuracy and computation and robustness to mismatched distributions. In J2, I developed a new class of models (ERMs) that considers the available level of data resolution in the training data, and uses the concept of contention at the bottleneck. ERMs were shown to rival the accuracy of flow line models, while requiring approximately 250 times less computation.
